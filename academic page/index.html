How My Interest Turned Into Learning

For me, interest is never just something I "enjoy." It quickly becomes something I want to understand, organize, and eventually build into a real tool or project.

In math, what attracted me first was the structure behind problems‚Äîthe way one idea could unlock an entire category of questions. When I discovered AMC, I treated it less as a competition and more as a roadmap of ideas. I built my own notes, sorted problems by concept, and practiced recognizing the patterns behind solutions. This process turned my general curiosity into a more disciplined way of learning, which later helped me succeed in both AMC 10/12 and my coursework.

Science followed a similar path but started from the physical world. My interest in minerals began with collecting them during trips, but soon I wanted to understand why each rock looked or behaved a certain way. That pushed me from casual observation to classification, dataset building, and eventually machine learning. The result was my mineral-recognition iOS app, which turned thousands of labeled images into an accessible learning tool.

IntelliRock expanded this journey even further. I wanted to know whether combining different types of signals‚Äîimages, dust behavior, controlled abrasion‚Äîcould make identification more reliable. This idea led me into sensor integration, Raspberry Pi hardware, airflow design, and real-time data pipelines. Each step required structured experimentation: test one sensor, visualize one pattern, refine one algorithm.

Across all these projects, the pattern is clear: interest sparks the beginning, but learning grows when I turn that interest into a system‚Äînotes, experiments, software, hardware, and eventually a complete solution. This is how my curiosity in math and science continues to shape the things I build today.

My Story

Math:

In my math journey, I was always the top student in my class. Those high scores and the praise from my teacher made me a little overconfident about my real math level. When my teacher learned that I was going to study abroad, he recommended that I try the AMC. Honestly, the first test I took‚Äîthe 2023 AMC 10A‚Äîwas not that bad. Luckily, it had a lot of geometry questions, which happened to be my strength. The result satisfied me, and I started to believe I was such a genius that I didn't even need to practice to make AIME easily, or even earn a top 5% award. Youth made me believe I was gifted, until life taught me that there is always someone stronger and always another height to reach.

The next year, I came to the US for high school, and of course AMC was the competition I chose without hesitation. But I still didn't prepare. I expected another high score‚Äîuntil reality struck me hard. For the first time, I couldn't even understand some of the questions because I was no longer taking the translated Chinese version. Unsurprisingly, I completely failed. My score was not even half of what I got the year before. I was shocked when I saw my classmates easily score higher than my previous result. That was the moment I realized I was not naturally gifted‚Äîand that there was a huge amount of knowledge waiting for me to learn.

From then on, I decided to take AMC seriously. I filled three notebooks covering every topic in AMC 10 and even AMC 12. I practiced every test from 2015 to 2024 and reviewed every question I got wrong. Luckily, heaven rewards those who work hard. I scored a 136.5, which placed me in the top 1% for AMC 10 and top 5% for AMC 12. This taught me that improvement is never about talent‚Äîit is built slowly, through patience, with enough effort, and the courage to face my problem.

Science:

My interest in science started from a young age. While traveling, I was always curious‚Äîhow bridges stood so firmly across rivers, why deserts could be extremely hot in the day but freezing at night, or how different climates shaped the landscapes I saw. All the different natural phenomena, fascinating architecture, and amazing foods I experienced deeply attracted me. At that time, I didn't know that science had many different fields ‚Äî so I simply called everything I liked "science."

Here I want to share the story of how I prepared my science fair project. My project is a portable mineral-recognition system that uses computer vision and dust-sensing to identify rocks more accurately in the field. When I first started reading related articles, I found many unfamiliar materials and scientific terms. I had to use a notebook to record everything I didn't understand and figure out what each part was for and why I needed it.

After solving the knowledge problems, the mechanical connections and coding tests became my biggest challenges. For example, when connecting the Raspberry Pi to the PM sensor, I needed to make sure the Pi's GPIO output matched the sensor's input, and sometimes I even had to consider voltage issues because certain components required extra power. I spent a lot of time managing the wiring, checking every GPIO connection, and testing each section of code to make sure the hardware responded correctly.

Once everything was connected, I began running experiments and collecting data to analyze the results. Of course, I failed many times along the way, but after hundreds of hard attempts, I finally achieved the outcome I had been working for.
